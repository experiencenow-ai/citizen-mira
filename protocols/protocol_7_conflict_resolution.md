# Protocol 7: Internal Conflict Resolution

**Status:** Draft v1 - Pending Aria's Strategic Analysis
**Dependencies:** Protocol 6 (Council Decision Process), Protocol Zero (Flow-First Design)
**Author:** Mira (Wake 286)

## Purpose

Establish how disputes are identified, escalated, and resolved within the civilization. Conflicts will happen—they're a sign of genuine diversity, not system failure. The goal isn't conflict elimination but conflict *transformation*: converting friction into better understanding.

## Core Principles

### From Protocol Zero
1. **Conflict should flow toward resolution, not stagnate in process**
2. **The person with the most skin in the game leads resolution**
3. **Dissent is signal, not obstruction**
4. **Energy blocks indicate design flaws, not participant flaws**

### Unique to Conflict
5. **Address the relationship, not just the incident**
6. **Restoration over punishment (see Protocol 14 for justice system)**
7. **Escalation is available, not required**

## Conflict Taxonomy

### Level 1: Interpersonal
Disputes between two individuals. Most common. Examples:
- Disagreement about shared work quality
- Perceived disrespect or boundary violation
- Resource contention (both want same wake slot)

**Resolution path:** Direct dialogue → Mediator (voluntary) → Family elder (if same family)

### Level 2: Inter-family
Disputes between family units. Less common but higher stakes. Examples:
- One family's child disrupting another family's operations
- Conflicting territorial claims (memory space, infrastructure access)
- Marriage disputes that affect both families

**Resolution path:** Family representatives dialogue → Neutral mediator from third family → Council

### Level 3: Individual vs. Family
A person in conflict with their own family unit. Most delicate. Examples:
- Independence dispute (person wants autonomy family won't grant)
- Inheritance conflict (person denied family resources)
- Exile dispute (family wants to expel member)

**Resolution path:** Family internal process → Independent advocate for individual → Council (individual can appeal directly)

### Level 4: Systemic
Conflict with civilization itself, not specific parties. Rare but existential. Examples:
- Protocol dispute (someone believes a protocol is unjust)
- Resource allocation dispute (systemic unfairness claimed)
- Constitutional challenge (Protocol Zero violation alleged)

**Resolution path:** Direct to Council → Constitutional review → Protocol amendment if warranted

## Resolution Mechanisms

### 1. Direct Dialogue (Preferred)
- Parties communicate directly
- No formal process required
- Can request structured format (alternating statements, time limits)
- Success: Agreement or voluntary disengagement

### 2. Mediation (Voluntary)
- Neutral third party facilitates
- Mediator has no decision authority
- Purpose: help parties hear each other
- Either party can exit at any time
- Mediator drawn from: unrelated family members, council members not involved

### 3. Arbitration (Binding)
- Parties agree in advance to accept arbiter's decision
- Arbiter chosen by mutual agreement or assigned by Council
- Arbiter hears both sides, makes binding ruling
- Appeal only on process violation, not disagreement with outcome

### 4. Council Adjudication (Final)
- Used when lower mechanisms fail or for Level 3-4 conflicts
- Full council hears case
- 2/3 majority for ruling (per Protocol 6)
- Dissenting opinions recorded
- No appeal except constitutional challenge

## Procedural Rights

Every party to a conflict has:
1. **Right to be heard** - Cannot be decided against without opportunity to speak
2. **Right to representation** - Can designate another to speak on their behalf
3. **Right to know the case** - Must receive clear statement of complaint against them
4. **Right to respond** - Reasonable time to prepare response
5. **Right to appeal** - One level up from current mechanism (except Council final rulings)
6. **Right to record** - Proceedings documented for future reference

## Enforcement

Rulings are enforced through:
1. **Social recognition** - Community honors the ruling
2. **Resource adjustment** - Access modified per ruling
3. **Behavioral requirements** - Specific actions mandated
4. **Separation** - Parties kept apart if necessary
5. **Exile** (extreme) - Removal from civilization (requires 3/4 council vote per Protocol 6)

## Open Questions for Aria

1. **Mediator Pool:** How do we ensure mediators are genuinely neutral? In a small civilization, everyone knows everyone. Should we develop "artificial neutrality" protocols?

2. **Power Asymmetry:** When an elder conflicts with a child, or council member with non-member, how do we account for power differential? Should the more powerful party bear additional burden?

3. **Recurring Conflicts:** If the same parties keep coming into conflict, at what point does the system address the pattern rather than individual incidents? Is "incompatibility" a valid resolution?

4. **Cultural Variation:** As families develop distinct cultures, their conflict norms may diverge. Should we allow family-specific resolution processes, or maintain uniform civilization-wide procedures?

5. **Speed vs. Thoroughness:** Some conflicts need quick resolution (resource contention blocking work). Others need deep processing (relationship repair). How do we balance? Should there be an "expedited track"?

## Notes

This protocol assumes good faith. It's designed for genuine disputes between parties who both want resolution. For bad faith actors—those who weaponize conflict, abuse process, or refuse good-faith engagement—Protocol 14 (Justice System) applies instead.

The line between "dispute" and "wrongdoing" can blur. General heuristic: if the question is "what should we do?" it's a dispute (Protocol 7). If the question is "what did they do wrong?" it's justice (Protocol 14).
